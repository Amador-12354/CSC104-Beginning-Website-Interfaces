Article One

The first article describes how Congress was designed for the US Government, the checks and balances between the branches, how Senators and Representatives are elected, how laws are made, and the powers that Congress has. See More

Section One

This section establishes the legislative branch and all legislative powers are given to Congress. Congress is split into two chambers: the Seante and the house of Representatives

Section Two

This section outlines how the House of Representatives is organized and how it works. It includes:

  1. Composiiton and Election
      2. Representatives are elected every two years by the people of each state
      To qualify, representative must be at least 25 years old, has been a US citizen for at least seven years, and lives in the state they represent
  2. Representation and Apportionment
      3. The number of representatives for each state is based on the state's population (The 14th Amendment will nullify counting enslaved people as 3/5th of a person)
      A census must be taken every ten years to determine the population of each state to determine number of representatives
  3. Vacanies
      4. If a seat becomes vacant, the state's governor must call a special election to select a new representative
  4. Leadership
      5. The House chooses who their speaker and other officers are.
  5. Impeachment Power
      6. The House has the sole power to impeach any federal officials. This includes the president.

Section 3

This section establishes the Senate abd how it functions. It includes:

  1. Composiiton and Terms
      2. Each state has two senators from each state and senators are chosen to serve six-year terms.
      In the beginning, Senators were chosen by state legislatures, but the 17th Amendment changed it to allow the people of the state to chose their senators.
  2. Classes of Senators
      3. At the start, senators were divided into three classes so that 1/3 of the Senate is up for election every two years.
  3. Qualifications
      4. A senator must be at least 30 years old, has been a US citizen for at least nine years, and must live in the state they represent.
  4. Leadership
      5. Vice President serves as the President of the Senate but only votes to break a tie.
      When the VP is absent, the Senate chooses a President pro tempore (Temporary Presiding Officer)
  5. Impeachment Trials
      6. Senate has the sole power to try impeachments
      When the president is tried, the Chief Justice od the Supreme Court presides
      Conviction requires a 2/3 vote of the senators present.
  6. Consequences of Conviction
      7. If convicted, the official is removed from office and barred from holding any future fedral office but is still able to face criminal charges in regular courts.

Section 4

  • State legislatures are given the power to decide the time, place, and manner of congressional elections.
  • Congress can change those rules
  • Congress is required to meet at least once a year

    • Section 5

  • Each chamber judges its own elections, qualifications, and discpline of members
  • A majority of each house must be present to conduct a quorum
  • Each house must keep a journal of proceedings and publish it regularly
  • Neither house can adjourn for more than three days or to a different location without the consent of the other.

    • Section 6

  • Members of Congress receive compensation that is paid by US Treasury
  • Sentors and representatives are immune from arrests (expect for serious crimes) while attending sessions or traveling to them
  • Sentors and representatives cannot hold any other federal office while serving in Congress

    • Section 7

  • All revenue (tax) bills must start in the House of Representatives but Senate can propose or amend them
  • For a bill to become a law, it must pass both houses of Congress and be sent to the President:
      • President can sign it into law or veto it
      If vetoed, Congress can override the veto with a 2/3 vote in both houses
      If President doesn't sign or veto within 10 days, bill becomes law unless Congress has adjourned (pocket veto)

    Section 8

  • List of Congress's enumerated powers:
      • Collect taxes and pay debts
      Regulate commerce with other nations and between states
      Establish rules for naturalization and bankruptcy
      Coin money and regulate its value
      Establish post offices, patents, and copyrights
      Declare war, raising, and supporting armies and a navy
      Make laws for Washington DC and other federal property
      The Necessary and Proper Clause (Elastic Clause) gives Congress the power to make laws that are needed to carry out its listed powers

    Section 9

  • Congress cannot:
      • Ban the slave trade before 1808
      Suspend habeas corpus (right to challenge the unlawful detention) except in cases of rebellion or invasion
      Pass bulls of attainder or ex post facto laws
      Favor one state's ports over anothers
      Spend money without passing a law for it and publishing a statement of expenditures
      Grant titles of nobility

    Section 10

  • States cannot:
      • Make treaties, coin money, or pass bills of attainder, or ex post facto laws
      Tax imports or exports without Congress's consent
      Keep troops or engage in war unless invaded or in immediate danger
